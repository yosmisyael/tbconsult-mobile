Tuberculosis (TB) is caused by a bacterium called Mycobacterium tuberculosis. The bacteria usually attack the lungs, but TB bacteria can attack any part of the body such as the kidney, spine, and brain.

Common symptoms of active lung TB are:
- A cough that lasts 3 weeks or longer
- Coughing up blood or sputum (phlegm from deep inside the lungs)
- Chest pain
- Weakness or fatigue
- Weight loss
- Fever and chills
- Sweating at night

Treatment for drug-susceptible TB disease consists of a 6-month regimen of four antimicrobial drugs: isoniazid, rifampicin, ethambutol, and pyrazinamide. 
Patients must strictly follow their medication schedule, often overseen by a healthcare worker at a DOTS (Directly Observed Treatment, Short-course) center.

If a patient stops taking the drugs before the regimen is complete, or if they take the drugs incorrectly, the surviving TB bacteria can develop resistance, leading to Multidrug-Resistant TB (MDR-TB), which requires much longer and more complex treatment.
